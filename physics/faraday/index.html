Electromagnetic Induction

Faraday's Law of Induction is a basic law of electromagnetism predicting how a magnetic field will interact with an electric circuit to produce an electromotive force (EMF). This phenomenon is known as electromagnetic induction.

The Discovery

In 1831, Michael Faraday discovered that by moving a magnet through a coil of wire, he could "induce" an electric current. This discovery is the fundamental principle behind electric generators, transformers, and induction motors.

1. Faraday's Law

The induced electromotive force in any closed circuit is equal to the negative of the time rate of change of the magnetic flux enclosed by the circuit.

\[ \mathcal{E} = -N \frac{d\Phi_B}{dt} \]

2. Lenz's Law

The negative sign in the formula represents Lenz's Law. It states that the direction of the induced current is always such that it creates a magnetic field that opposes the change in magnetic flux that produced it.

3. Factors Affecting Induced EMF

  • Number of Turns (N): EMF is directly proportional to the number of loops in the coil.
  • Magnetic Field Strength: A stronger magnet increases the flux density.
  • Speed of Movement: The faster the magnet moves, the higher the rate of change of flux (\(dt\) becomes smaller), leading to a higher voltage.
