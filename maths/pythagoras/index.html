The Pythagorean Theorem

The Pythagorean Theorem is one of the most fundamental principles in Euclidean geometry. It describes the relationship between the three sides of a right-angled triangle.

1. The Theorem Statement

For a right-angled triangle:

\[ a^2 + b^2 = c^2 \]

where 'a' and 'b' are the lengths of the legs, and 'c' is the length of the hypotenuse.

The hypotenuse is always the longest side, located opposite the right angle ($90^\circ$). The theorem states that the area of the square built upon the hypotenuse is equal to the sum of the areas of the squares upon the remaining sides.

2. Historical Context

Although named after the Greek mathematician Pythagoras (c. 570–495 BC), the relationship was known to earlier civilizations, including the Babylonians and Indians, centuries before. However, Pythagoras is credited with the first recorded proof.

3. Geometric Proof

Imagine a large square with side length $(a+b)$. Inside this square, place four identical right triangles with sides $a, b, c$.

  • The area of the large square is $(a+b)^2$.
  • The four triangles take up area $4 \times \frac{1}{2}ab = 2ab$.
  • The remaining space in the center forms a square with side $c$ and area $c^2$.
  • Therefore, $(a+b)^2 = c^2 + 2ab$. Expanding this: $a^2 + 2ab + b^2 = c^2 + 2ab$. Subtracting $2ab$ from both sides gives $a^2 + b^2 = c^2$.

4. Pythagorean Triples

A Pythagorean triple consists of three positive integers $a, b, c$, such that $a^2 + b^2 = c^2$. Some common examples include:

  • (3, 4, 5): $9 + 16 = 25$
  • (5, 12, 13): $25 + 144 = 169$
  • (8, 15, 17): $64 + 225 = 289$

These integer sets are invaluable in carpentry and construction for ensuring perfect right angles.

5. Real-World Applications

The theorem is used in various fields:

  • Navigation: Calculating the shortest distance between two points (triangulation).
  • Construction: Ensuring corners are square (e.g., using a 3-4-5 rope).
  • Physics: Resolving vectors into components (velocity, force).
  • Computer Graphics: Calculating distances between pixels or objects in 3D space.

6. The Distance Formula

In a coordinate plane, the distance $d$ between two points $(x_1, y_1)$ and $(x_2, y_2)$ is derived directly from Pythagoras:

\[ d = \sqrt{(x_2 - x_1)^2 + (y_2 - y_1)^2} \]
